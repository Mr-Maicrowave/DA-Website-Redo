# Anna Nguyen

## Appreciation

I used to be so bad at maths that I would tell everyone it was my worst subject, and I *hated* it. Then I was placed in Ms Amanda’s class. She didn’t just teach maths. She changed the way I thought about it. She made me stop saying it was my worst subject and helped me see that I *could* improve. Her teaching style was unique, inspiring, and so easy to understand that the lessons just *stuck*.  

Trust me, having the right tutor changes everything. My cousin introduced me to DA, swearing by how a great tutor who cares and knows their stuff can change your marks. And she was right. Ms Amanda was strict, and after my first lesson, I honestly thought, “This is too hard for me.” But thank goodness I listened to my parents and stuck with it. Ms Amanda turned me from a student aiming *not to fail* to someone who refuses to settle for anything less than first place or 100%.  

Here’s what I learned the hard way: you can’t pick and choose what advice to follow. At first, I only listened to what I *thought* was right, and whilst I improved, I wasn’t topping the grade.  

Then I started paying attention to the top kids in my class at DA. What were they doing differently? They *listened.* They followed every piece of advice their tutors gave them and did things that made a real difference: They did all their homework. They practised every exam question given by her. They prioritised the important things. They stayed focused, enjoyed every lesson and loved coming to class.  

So I made changes. I also took on her advice of cutting out distractions—no more gaming or scrolling on my phone until I’d finished my work. I started treating games as a reward, not a habit. My phone? I only used it when necessary. These small changes freed up so much of my time and gave me the space to focus on what mattered most.

## Advice for current students

If there’s one piece of advice I can give you, it’s this: take responsibility for your marks and your actions. Blaming others, making excuses, or pointing to circumstances will only keep you stuck in the same habits and with the same results.  

That used to be me before I joined DA. I always had an excuse for my parents about why I failed a test. But after a deep, honest talk with Ms Amanda, I learned to stop making excuses and start taking accountability. I reflected on where things went wrong: I was cramming for exams at the last minute, I wasn’t practising enough exam questions, and I wasn’t putting in consistent effort.  

Admitting my mistakes was hard, but it allowed me to learn from them and avoid repeating them. Slowly, my marks began to improve, and my confidence grew.  

Another key lesson? Don’t always follow your feelings. Do what’s needed and what’s right. It’s easy to stick with a tutor because they’re friendly and make you feel comfortable, but comfort doesn’t always equal progress. My old tutor was nice and friendly, but I wasn’t improving. I stayed in my comfort zone, convinced that I was just bad at the subject. Sometimes, pressure is necessary to succeed. Discipline is required to grow out of bad habits. A tough, fair, caring, and smart teacher makes all the difference. They push you to do better, hold you accountable, and help you become the best version of yourself.
