Want to Be the Best? Here’s How Top Students Do It

Coming first in one exam is tough. Coming first in every assessment? That’s the stuff of champions. You know who I’m talking about: the students who always seem to score 100%, as if success flows through their veins. But the truth is, it’s not magic, and it’s definitely not luck.

How badly do you want it? Success doesn’t happen by chance; it’s earned through hard work. Those top students? They’re not superhuman. They simply maximise every tool, every resource, and every moment they have. So, what will you do with yours?

Are you using the help provided to its full potential?

Are you spending your time where it matters most?

Are you completing every task, every practice, every revision like it counts?

Are you taking your tutor’s advice and turning it into action?

Are you fully engaged, learning with focus and curiosity to succeed?

Are you treating every lesson, every concept, and every opportunity to learn with the seriousness it deserves?

The reason they succeed often lies in these questions. The difference isn’t talent. It’s discipline, preparation, and consistency.

We’ve gathered insights from top-performing students across schools and grades in both Maths and English. Students who don’t just come first in exams, but dominate them, achieving perfect scores time and time again. They’re not just chasing excellence—they’re defining it. These quiet achievers and relentless workers prove that perfection isn’t a gift, rather it’s a craft. It’s built on habits, practices, and unwavering dedication. Their success might look effortless, but it’s powered by systems that leave nothing to chance.

And here’s the best part: they’ve shared the exact strategies that helped them rise—practical steps you can start applying today. Because success isn’t a distant dream. It’s a choice you make every single day—to learn, to grow, to adapt, and to push beyond where you are right now. It’s in the small habits, the focused effort, and the refusal to let setbacks define you that champions are made.

Are you ready to stop watching others win and start claiming your own success? This isn’t just their moment. It’s yours. Step up, show up, and let’s make it happen.

Here’s what I learned the hard way: you can’t pick and choose what advice to follow. At first, I only listened to what I thought was right, and whilst I improved, I wasn’t topping the grade.

Then I started paying attention to the top kids in my class at DA. What were they doing differently? They listened. They prioritised the right things. They showed up to every lesson and loved coming to class.

So I made changes. I also took her advice of cutting out distractions—no more gaming or scrolling on my phone until I’d finished my work. I started treating games as a reward, not a habit. My phone? I only used it when necessary. These small changes freed up so much of my time and gave me the space to focus on what mattered most.

Advice for Current Students

If there’s one piece of advice I can give you, it’s this: take responsibility for your marks and your actions. Blaming others, making excuses, or pointing to circumstances will only keep you stuck in the same habits and with the same results.

That used to be me before I joined DA. I always had an excuse for my parents about why I failed a test. But after a deep, honest talk with Ms Amanda, I learned to stop making excuses and start taking accountability. I reflected on where things went wrong: I was cramming for exams at the last minute, I wasn’t practising enough exam questions, and I wasn’t putting in consistent effort.

Admitting my mistakes was hard, but it allowed me to learn from them and avoid repeating them. Slowly, my marks began to improve, and my confidence grew.

Another key lesson? Don’t always follow your feelings. Do what’s needed and what’s right. It’s easy to stick with a tutor because they’re friendly and make you feel comfortable, but comfort doesn’t always equal progress. My old tutor was nice and friendly, but I wasn’t improving. I stayed in my comfort zone, convinced that I was just bad at the subject. Sometimes, pressure is necessary to succeed. Discipline is required to grow out of bad habits. A tough, fair, caring, and smart teacher makes all the difference. They push you to do better, hold you accountable, and help you become the best version of yourself.
