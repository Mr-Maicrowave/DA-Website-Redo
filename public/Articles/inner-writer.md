Unlocking Your Child’s Inner Writer: Tips for Success

Every parent has seen it: the sigh, the sluggish pencil grip, and the blank page that feels like an insurmountable hurdle. You beg, fall… maybe even raise your voice – and they write even slower… Twenty minutes later, there’s just one sentence on a blank paper. Writing can often feel like a monumental task, especially for reluctant young writers. But what if we told you there’s a way to transform your child’s dread into confidence and creativity?

As parents and educators, we have the unique opportunity to turn writing from a daunting chore into an exciting adventure. Here are research-backed tips to help your child unlock their inner writer.

The Real Challenge: Starting the Journey

“The problem with writing is not poor spelling, punctuation, grammar and handwriting. The problem with writing is no writing.”

Many students falter in English because they feel unmotivated or overwhelmed. Writing is still a cornerstone of academic success. Yet, for students, the blank page represents a mountain they feel they’ll never climb.

Making Writing Relatable

Connect writing to your child’s interests and experiences using real-world approaches. Is your child passionate about video games, a certain movie, hobby or a recent family trip? Encourage them to write about it!

Personal connections fuel motivation and creativity. Let them choose the format that suits them best – a poem, a short story, or even a comic strip. By giving your child choice over their writing, you empower them to take ownership of their writing journey, making the process more enjoyable and meaningful.

One of the best ways to help reluctant writers is to encourage them to read, listen, and discuss their ideas before they put pen to paper. At home, spark discussions and ask open questions allowing them to articulate their thoughts and ideas.

Reading and discussing ideas for writing not only enhances their understanding, it also provides writers with the target language to express and clarify their thoughts, and activates their ideas to make the writing task more accessible when they begin.

Multimodal communication such as movies, clips, images and audio can motivate disengaged students, inspiring them to read and write.

Embrace Technology

Contrary to what a majority of parents believe, technology isn’t the enemy—it’s a powerful ally. Today’s students thrive in digital spaces, so why not meet them there? Writing doesn’t have to be limited to essays.

A child who dreads pen-and-paper tasks might love creating a Spotify podcast or crafting a TikTok for the fun of it or to share their unique perspective with the world.

Integrating technology to our writing tasks can make learning more meaningful by providing students with opportunities to investigate, experiment, document, design, visualise and communicate (Jonassen et al., 2008).

Tech tools also make revising less intimidating. Writing is a process, and apps or online platforms help students draft, edit, gather feedback from teachers and peers, and refine their work. This ease of revision encourages better clarity and fluency, helping your child see writing as an evolving skill rather than a one-shot effort.

Revision is vital, helping students organise ideas, strengthen arguments, and refine their voice, yet many students skip revision due to anxiety, laziness, ignorance, time constraints, “attitude” or a lack of understanding of its significance.

Research by Williams & Beam (2019) shows students can develop as writers if they understand and regularly practice going through the writing process, developing their writing skills and strategies.

Celebrate Progress, Not Perfection

Great writing takes time—just ask J.K. Rowling, who spent 5 years revising Harry Potter (even experienced writers take years to refine their story!). Help your child understand that writing isn’t about getting it perfect on the first try. Encourage them to experiment, reflect, and revise.

A Real-Life Success Story

Take Katherine Phung, for example. Once a hesitant Year 4 writer, she blossomed through consistent encouragement and tailored strategies. I remember the days when her mother would complain about her D average in English. I would need to dictate sentence starters, and sometimes entire sentences, to help inspire her.

With the unwavering trust of her cooperative parents and the consistent application of these strategies, Katherine’s writing abilities flourished.

Katherine overcame her aversion to writing and excelled, eventually achieving 2nd place in the Reading to Write preliminary exam, and 1st place in her final exam essay for Module B Close Study of Literature at Canley Vale High School.

Her journey shows how the right approach can transform even the most reluctant writer into a top performer.

Writing: A Lifelong Skill

By connecting writing to your child’s world, using technology as a tool, and celebrating their growth, you can turn writing from a dreaded task into a powerful form of self-expression.

The key is starting small, encouraging effort, and showing them that their words matter.
